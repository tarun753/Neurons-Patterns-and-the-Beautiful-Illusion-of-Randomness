Neurons, Patterns, and the Beautiful Illusion of Randomness

A semi-formal essay with experiments you can try, a few equations, and a selective bibliography

By Tarun Kumar Mahato ¬∑ Last updated: 12 Aug 2025 ¬∑ ~12 minute read

Your thoughts feel spontaneous. Under the hood they are structured walks through memory. Brains are generative models that predict the next moment, then update when surprised. What feels random is usually a recombination of learned features with a touch of noise. Mechanism doesn‚Äôt erase meaning; it explains how meaning survives. AI mirrors this pattern logic‚Äîso if you understand the mechanism you can steer it, which is the closest thing we have to free will.

1. Why this matters

You think of a purple penguin, or an impossible fruit that tastes like pizza. It feels like a spark from nowhere. Yet the spark usually lands on dry tinder that your nervous system has been piling up for years. This is a good thing. Without well structured memory you would not be able to think at all. The live question is simple: how much of everyday creativity is recombination of stored parts, and how much is true novelty? The honest answer is that most of it is recombination, sometimes guided by small doses of noise. That is already enough to look like magic from the inside.

Key idea: A thought is a path through a graph of memories, not a bolt from the void.

2. The brain by the numbers (finite but astronomical)

  • Roughly 86 billion neurons, each with thousands of synapses.
  • Synapses change their strengths with experience.
  • The whole network has small‚Äëworld structure. Short paths exist between distant regions, which makes association fast and surprisingly rich.

Even absurdly large is still finite. That matters. A finite system cannot produce unbounded novelty. It can produce enormous variability by recombining elements it already contains.

3. What ‚Äúrandom‚Äù really means here

Mathematicians talk about algorithmic randomness. A sequence looks random if it cannot be compressed by a shorter description. Your thoughts usually compress well: they borrow from categories, prototypes, and stories you already have. When you try to be random, you often fail predictable tests. Human ‚Äúrandom‚Äù tends to over‚Äëavoid runs (people underproduce streaks like HHHHH in coin flip guesses) and to cluster around recency and salience.

Try it: write 200 digits that you claim are random. Now run a compressor on the string. If it shrinks, your digits had patterns you did not notice. Your mind protects you from entropy. That is its job.

4. Brains as generative models

A compact picture of modern neuroscience: the brain continually predicts the next sensory input, then updates when the world disagrees. This is often called predictive processing or the free energy view. In symbols:

$$\text{posterior} \propto \text{prior} \times \text{likelihood}$$

You carry priors learned from life. Perception and thought are inferences that reconcile those priors with incoming data. Creativity is the same engine running off‚Äëdistribution, with more latitude and more search.

Practical knob: arousal, attention, curiosity, caffeine, mood, and context change how tight your priors are. Looser priors widen the search space and make unusual combinations more likely.

5. The ‚Äúnew face you cannot quite invent‚Äù experiment

Close your eyes and conjure a face that has never existed. You will feel your visual system snapping to familiar axes: eye spacing, nose width, hairline shape, skin texture. Computer vision calls this low‚Äëdimensional face space. New faces are often linear blends of known faces. That is why your ‚Äúalien‚Äù still looks suspiciously Earthlike. The brain‚Äôs ventral stream leans on feature codes learned from the statistics of the world.

Takeaway: what you call spontaneity is usually structured recombination along learned feature directions.

6. Deep nets as a mirror

Large models write poems and paint images that look novel. Under the hood they optimize a probability model over tokens or pixels. Each parameter is a small piece of pattern memory. This is why human creativity and model output feel parallel. In both cases, there is:

  • a vast library of features,
  • a search over combinations,
  • and a scoring function that prefers coherent results.

People add two extra loops that matter: metacognition (you can think about your own thinking) and values (you decide what is worth producing). The mirror is real, but it is not perfect.

7. A toy model of ‚Äúrandom thought‚Äù

Imagine memory as a graph. Nodes are traces, edges are associations. Thinking is a biased random walk:

  • Start at the current context node $c$.
  • Jump to a neighbor with probability proportional to edge weight and priming.
  • Occasionally jump farther with small probability $\epsilon$ to simulate distraction, sensation, or noise.

Even with noise, the next step depends on where you were. So the conditional probability $P(\text{next} \,|\, \text{history})$ is not independent. That dependence is the fingerprint of pattern. If you log your words over a minute, you will see semantic drift along meaningful lines: lamp ‚Üí desk ‚Üí study ‚Üí exam ‚Üí relief. A purely random process would not cluster like that.

8. Emotion and the chemistry of meaning

Falling in love, heartbreak, awe, grief. These states ride on neurotransmitters and circuit motifs. Dopamine tracks prediction errors and drives learning. Oxytocin tunes trust and bonding. Serotonin links to mood and patience. None of this cheapens the experience. It shows you why it hits as hard as it does, and why certain contexts amplify it. You are not replacing poetry with plumbing. You are discovering the pipes that let poetry flow.

Human side note: explain a hug by ions and you still want the hug. Explanation is not erasure. Meaning survives the microscope.

9. Where true randomness could enter

Neurons are noisy at the ion channel level. Networks amplify or damp that noise depending on state. Some theorists argue for quantum contributions. Even if some noise is truly random, that does not create will by itself. Randomness without structure is just a glitch. Useful creativity is structured search plus a little turbulence, not turbulence alone.

10. Small experiments you can run today

  1. The one minute timer. Every 20 seconds write the first word you think of. Draw arrows between them. Notice the themed clusters and returns.
  2. Semantic walk. Start from ‚Äúbanana‚Äù. Write ten words by association only. Now start from ‚Äújustice‚Äù. Compare the geometry of the two walks.
  3. Compression test. Generate 200 digits you claim are random. Check how many simple patterns show up (repeats, alternations, local runs).
  4. Priming flip. Read a short list: ocean, blue, salt, wave, gull. Now do the one minute timer. Your first few words will tilt maritime. That tilt is your priors in action.

11. How to use this in real life

  • If you want new ideas, change the graph. Expose yourself to new domains, not just more of the same feed.
  • If you want depth, tighten the priors. Focus, reduce noise, and climb the hill of one concept until it yields something precise.
  • If you want surprising combinations, loosen the priors. Walk farther in the graph before judging.
  • If you want meaning, attach values. Decide what counts as a good answer before you start searching.

12. Closing

You can say that a mother‚Äôs hug is spikes and synapses. You can be correct. The hug still matters. The chemistry explains the how, not the why. Meaning lives in the patterns that survive prediction, in the commitments you keep, in the stories you endorse when no one is looking. The wonder is that matter can host any of this at all.

One sentence to remember: not pure randomness, but an astonishing engine of recombinative order that feels like freedom from the inside.
